> "When learning English, you should start with conversation, not grammar."

There's a lot of truth to this. Many learners spend years studying grammar rules and vocabulary lists but struggle to hold a real conversation when the moment comes. Conversation-first approaches — like immersion, language exchange, or apps like Duolingo — build the intuitive feel for a language that grammar study alone rarely delivers.

That said, "start with conversation, not grammar" works best as a guiding principle, not an absolute rule. A small foundation of grammar helps you form basic sentences and avoid deeply ingrained errors that become hard to unlearn later. Complete immersion without any structural scaffolding can leave beginners frustrated and stuck.

The strongest evidence for conversation-first comes from how children acquire their first language — through interaction, not textbooks. Adults aren't children, but the principle holds: language is fundamentally a social tool, and practicing it socially from early on accelerates fluency in ways that passive study doesn't.

So the advice is largely sound, especially as a corrective for learners who over-study grammar and under-practice speaking. The key nuance is "start with," not "replace entirely." A balanced approach leans heavily toward conversation while using grammar as a support structure, not the foundation.

### Conclusion

**Revision needed.**

Two counter-arguments stand:

1. **Error fossilization without feedback (CA #2):** The judgment is valid when conversational practice includes corrective feedback, but the Turn 1 response did not make this condition explicit. A learner could reasonably read the advice as endorsing unsupervised conversation — which carries real fossilization risk.

2. **Goal dependency (CA #3):** The claim as stated applies universally to "learning English," but conversation-first is not the best approach for all English learning goals. Academic, legal, or writing-focused learners may benefit more from grammar-first approaches.

**Revised judgment:** For learners whose goal is communicative fluency, conversation-first (with corrective feedback) is strongly supported. But the original claim is over-broad — it should be scoped to conversational fluency goals, and it should specify that feedback mechanisms are necessary to mitigate fossilization risk.

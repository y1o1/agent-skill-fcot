> "Remote work is more productive than office work."

The productivity comparison between remote and office work is genuinely nuanced — the evidence doesn't support a blanket verdict either way.

**Remote work advantages are real:** Eliminating commutes reclaims time and energy. Workers report higher autonomy and fewer interruptions from drop-by conversations. Stanford economist Nicholas Bloom's research found a roughly 13% productivity increase among call center workers who shifted to remote work.

**But the picture is more complicated than that.** Bloom's own later research on fully remote knowledge workers found more mixed results. Collaboration-heavy tasks, mentorship, and creative brainstorming tend to suffer without in-person proximity. Junior employees, in particular, learn significantly less when removed from the office environment. Home settings vary enormously — a quiet home office is not the same as sharing a studio apartment.

**What the evidence actually suggests:**
- Productivity gains are most consistent for **focused, individual tasks**
- Productivity losses appear for **collaborative, creative, or learning-intensive work**
- **Hybrid models** (2–3 days in-office) consistently show the strongest outcomes in recent studies

So the original claim overstates it. Remote work *can* boost productivity under the right conditions, but "more productive than office work" as a universal statement isn't supported by the research.

**A more accurate framing:** Productivity depends on the nature of the work, the individual's home environment, and how well the team is structured for remote collaboration.
